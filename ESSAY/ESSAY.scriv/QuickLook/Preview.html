      The gothic calls for a large-scale examination.

    • general methods
      To take the first clumsy steps toward that large-scale examination, I have deployed data visualizations of metadata regarding 208 early Gothic texts. My data derives from Ann Tracy’s bibliography The Gothic Novel 1790-1830: Plot Summaries and Index to Motifs; my experimental method is informed by Lisa Samuels and Jerome McGann’s “Deformance and Interpretation” and by Willard McCarty’s “Knowing: Modeling in Literary Studies.”

  • Results

    • 70 pie charts
      So basically I made 70 pie charts.

    • biggest charts
      The defining features of the Gothic as a genre appear to be fainting, confinement, abduction, storm, and suicide mentions.

    • 39 neutral charts
      Only one of those 5 charts shows much of a contrast between men and women, which is in keeping with the bulk of my results. 39 of the 70 motifs-- 55% of them-- are distinguished by less than a 60-40 split, with three perfect ties. Of the 31 that show a notable contrast, only eight show a difference of more than 70-30.

    • 29 male charts
      Of those charts that did seem to indicate a gendered trend, 29 of them imdicated male dominance of largely the things we would expect men to dominate. These motifs suggest that the darkest side of the Gothic was primarily the province of men: death may be slightly female-dominated, but murder is male-dominated, as are blood, corpses, and bones.
